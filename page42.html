Rapid-Sequence 



• The goal of a rapid sequence induction is to minimize the time when neither the patient nor you can protect the patient’s airway. This is usually the time between induction (the time the patient loses control) and intubation (the time you gain control).
This is used for pateints that did not meet the NPO guidelines, patients that are > 10 weeks pregnant, significant gastro-esophageal-reflux disease, significant gastroparesis, trauma, or NPO but with a significant distracting injury (narcotics for treatment may slow bowel motility).

• Contraindication: A known or presumed difficult airway is an absolute contraindication to a rapid sequence intubation. If you induce anesthesia and paralyze the patient and then can’t intubate, the patient will be left with an uncontrolled airway long enough time to potentially aspirate.

• Indication: Any “full stomach” patient has an indication for a rapid sequence intubation. This is assuming that the case is emergent, and can’t be postponed or canceled.

• Make sure you check all equipment is present, checked, and on, including suction.

• Set up a second ETT tube one size smaller than the one the anesthesia provider is anticipating on using.

• Check all patient monitors. (EKG, NIBP, Oximitry, End-tidal CO2/CO2 detector)

• Pre-oxygenate / nitrogen washout: 100% oxygen by tight fitting mask; 5 deep breaths or 3 minutes normal breathing; this replaces all lung nitrogen with oxygen, thereby allowing a longer apnic period.

• Provide crichoid pressure. There is a video on how to do this on the Tutorials page.

• The anesthesia provider will start the induction process, and you will continue holding crichoid pressure while you wait until the paralytic has taken effect

• You’ll notice that they wont be mask ventilating the patient, because this may insuflate air into the stomach and increases the risk of vomiting/aspirating.

• Tube Placement: Once the tube is in, hold the tube at the lips of the patient. This prevents the tube from going in deeper, or even worse, coming out. Continue to hold th etube while the anesthesia provider checks for bilateral breath sounds, endtidal CO2, bilateral chest rise and fog in the ETT. Once the tube is taped or tied into position, or you are told to do so, you can let go of the tube.

• Only now may you stop the crichoid pressure.
