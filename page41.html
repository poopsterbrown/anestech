Normal Induction



• This assumes that the patient does not have a “Full Stomach” and that the patient does not have a known or suspected “difficult airway”.

• Equipment and Drugs checked, including the most important tool in anesthesia: the suction.

• Check all patient monitors. (EKG, NIBP, Oximitry, End-tidal CO2/CO2 detector)

• Pre-oxygenate / nitrogen washout: 100% oxygen by tight fitting mask; 5 deep breaths or 3 minutes normal breathing; this replaces all lung nitrogen with oxygen, thereby allowing a longer apnic period.

• Lidocaine: This is used to numb the patient’s veins since some of the following drugs can sting and/or burn.

• Fentanyl: This is typically the narcotic of choice. The purpose of this drug at this time is to lower the blood pressure. The most traumatic part of any surgery is the breathing tube being placed. We do not like things going down our throats, and this process is going to make the patient hypertensive. By giving a narcotic such as Fentanyl ahead of time, the goal is for the lowered blood pressure to be met with the increased blood pressure from the ET tube, to simply put the patient back to their baseline BP.

• Propofol or pentothal or inhalation induction is next by adding nitrous-oxide/sevoflurane to inhaled oxygen. We dose the IV induction agent high enough so that the patient will go to sleep, but also low enough so that it will wear off before the patient desaturates (pulse ox number dropping) in case we cannot ventilate.

• Bag mask the patient: with this we establish that we can actually bag and oxygenate/ventilate the patient before we give a paralytic. If we cannot bagmask the patient, we let the induction drug wear off and choose a different way of securing the airway.

• Paralysis: Typically succhinylcholine is used for the muscle relaxant because it’s a non-depolarizing. It gets dosed high enough so that we attain vocal cord paralysis, but also low enough so that it will wear off before the patient desaturates in case we cannot ventilate, thereby returning the patient to the previously established asleep-bag-mask state.

• At this point, all the drugs are given. We bag-mask the patient and establish that we can oxygenate/ventilate the now paralyzed patient. If we cannot bag-mask the patient at this point, we attempt an intubation or other means of establishing an airway. If we are unable to do so, we let the paralytic wear off and resume bag-masking until the patient wakes up. Then we can choose a different way of securing the airway.

• If we can bag-mask the patient, we proceed with definitive airway control. There is no rush, as we have established that we can oxygenate/ventilate the asleep and paralyzed patient. So if we cannot intubate the patient, we can at least bag him/her until we find another way to secure the airway or until the paralytic and then the induction drug wears off and the patient wakes up.

• Tube Placement: Once the tube is in, hold the tube at the lips of the patient. This prevents the tube from going in deeper, or even worse, coming out. Continue to hold th etube while the anesthesia provider checks for bilateral breath sounds, endtidal CO2, bilateral chest rise and fog in the ETT. Once the tube is taped or tied into position, or you are told to do so, you can let go of the tube.
